Salesforce thrives on teamwork, but one of the most challenging dynamics to manage is the relationship between **Salesforce developers and admins**. Both roles are critical to the success of any **Salesforce implementation**, yet they often operate in silos. Miscommunication, overlapping efforts, and differing approaches to problem-solving can lead to inefficiencies, delays, and a system that’s harder to maintain.

In this article, we’ll explore the common challenges between **Salesforce developers and admins**, why these issues arise, and actionable strategies to foster better collaboration. Whether you’re an admin working alongside developers or a developer navigating **Salesforce automation tools**, these tips will help you bridge the gap and work together more effectively.

* * *

## 5 Common Challenges Between Developers and Admins

#### **1\. Differing Problem-Solving Approaches**

Admins and developers often approach problems from different perspectives, which can lead to friction.

- **Admins** typically rely on **declarative Salesforce automation tools** like Flow, Dynamic Forms, and validation rules to configure business logic without writing code. These tools are quick to deploy and easier for non-technical teams to maintain.
    
- **Developers** prefer programmatic solutions using **Apex, Lightning Web Components (LWC), and Salesforce DevOps tools**, which offer greater flexibility and scalability for complex requirements.
    

Neither approach is inherently better, and both have their strengths. **Clicks vs. code** is an ongoing debate, as declarative tools are efficient but may struggle with advanced logic or large data volumes. Custom code provides more control but can lead to **technical debt** and maintenance challenges.

**How to Fix It:**  
The best solutions often combine the strengths of both approaches. **Salesforce developers and admins** should collaborate early in the process to determine the most effective tools for each task.

* * *

#### **2\. Poor Communication**

When **Salesforce developers and admins** work in silos, misunderstandings about **Salesforce project management, deployment strategies, and automation** can arise. Without clear documentation and regular communication, teams risk duplicating work, misaligning expectations, and wasting time on rework.

**How to Fix It:**  
Clear and consistent communication is essential for avoiding friction and building cohesive solutions. Here are some practical steps:

- Schedule regular check-ins to align on **Salesforce deployment strategies**.
    
- Use shared documentation tools like Confluence or Notion to track **Salesforce automation tools** and development efforts.
    
- Set up dedicated Slack or Chatter channels for quick discussions and troubleshooting.
    
- Ensure **Salesforce project management** provides visibility into all workstreams.
    

* * *

#### **3\. Security and Scalability Issues**

Admins and developers often have different perspectives on **Salesforce security and performance**, which can lead to conflicts.

- Admins may grant broad permissions for convenience, potentially exposing sensitive data.
    
- Developers might hardcode logic without considering future business changes, resulting in inflexible solutions that are difficult to scale.
    

**How to Fix It:**  
To address these concerns, teams should:

- Align on **Salesforce security best practices** to minimize risks.
    
- Follow Salesforce’s **least-privilege model** for permissions and automation.
    
- Encourage cross-learning to bridge knowledge gaps between teams.
    

* * *

#### **4\. Misaligned Deployment and Change Management**

Admins and developers often have different approaches to implementing changes in Salesforce, which can lead to deployment challenges.

- Admins may make direct changes in production to address urgent needs.
    
- Developers typically follow a structured **Salesforce deployment strategy**, using sandboxes, version control (Git), and **Salesforce DevOps tools** like Copado or Gearset to ensure proper testing and deployment.
    

This disconnect can result in overwritten changes, system errors, and version conflicts.

**How to Fix It:**  
To streamline **Salesforce deployment strategies**, teams should:

- Establish a shared deployment strategy that balances speed and stability.
    
- Encourage admins to use sandboxes for testing before deploying changes to production.
    
- Leverage **version control tools** like GitHub or Bitbucket and **Salesforce DevOps tools** to manage deployments effectively.
    

* * *

#### **5\. Lack of Project Management and Process Alignment**

Without clear processes, even the most skilled **Salesforce teams** can struggle to stay aligned. Poor **Salesforce project management** can lead to overlapping tasks, conflicting priorities, and rushed deployments.

Common issues include:

- Lack of visibility into upcoming work, resulting in duplicated or conflicting efforts.
    
- No **prioritization framework**, making it unclear which tasks should take precedence.
    
- No formal review process, leading to avoidable errors during deployment.
    

**How to Fix It:**  
Structured **Salesforce project management** can help teams work more efficiently and reduce surprises. Key steps include:

- Adopting **Agile or Kanban frameworks** using tools like Jira or Asana to track tasks.
    
- Implementing backlog grooming and sprint planning to align priorities.
    
- Defining a “definition of done” to ensure changes meet documentation and testing standards before deployment.
    

### 5 Strategies to Strengthen Collaboration

Here are five actionable strategies to improve teamwork and drive better outcomes:

#### **1\. Define When to Use Clicks vs. Code**

- Create a **decision matrix** to determine when **declarative Salesforce automation tools** or programmatic solutions are most appropriate.
    
- Adopt a “**clicks before code**” approach while recognizing when custom code is necessary.
    

#### **2\. Improve Communication**

- Hold regular sync meetings to align on **Salesforce project management**.
    
- Use collaborative documentation to track decisions and implementations.
    
- Leverage Slack or Chatter for quick troubleshooting.
    

#### **3\. Standardize Deployment and Change Management**

- Require admins to test changes in sandboxes before deploying to production.
    
- Use **Salesforce DevOps tools** and version control to manage deployments effectively.
    

#### **4\. Align on Security and Performance Best Practices**

- Conduct joint training sessions on **Salesforce security and performance**, governor limits, and optimization techniques.
    
- Ensure both teams follow Salesforce’s security guidelines for permissions and automation.
    

#### **5\. Encourage Cross-Skilling**

- Admins can learn basic Apex to better understand developer workflows.
    
- Developers can explore Flow and **Salesforce automation tools** to appreciate admin-driven solutions.
    

#### Conclusion

At the end of the day, **Salesforce developers and admins** share the same goal: building solutions that drive business success. The key is not to favor one approach over the other but to strike a balance between **clicks vs. code**, speed and stability, flexibility and control.

By aligning on **Salesforce best practices**, improving communication, and fostering a culture of collaboration, teams can break down silos and create scalable, secure, and maintainable solutions. When **Salesforce developers and admins** work together effectively, Salesforce becomes more than just a platform—it becomes a powerful enabler of business growth.
